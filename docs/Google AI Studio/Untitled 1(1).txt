> For years, researchers have struggled with a shortage of high-quality training data that could help AI models truly master mathematical reasoning. 
> 
> A new project tackles this by tapping into a surprising resource: decades of work in automated theorem proving. Instead of relying on today’s language models—which can make mistakes—or on highly technical proof systems like Lean and Isabelle, the team built a framework around E-prover, a symbolic reasoning engine, and the massive TPTP library of logical axioms.
> https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.06809
> 
> Their approach is straightforward but powerful: saturate axioms to generate new theorems, filter out the trivial ones, and turn the rest into reasoning tasks. Because this process is purely symbolic, every theorem produced is guaranteed correct, avoiding the factual errors that LLMs often introduce. The generated data is organized into three types of challenges—checking whether a claim follows from given premises, picking the right premises, and reconstructing proofs—with different levels of difficulty.
> 
> When state-of-the-art AI models were tested on these tasks, their performance sharply declined on problems that demanded deeper, structural reasoning rather than surface-level pattern recognition. This highlights a crucial gap in current AI: while they excel at language, they still stumble at rigorous mathematical logic. At the same time, there are limits to what this method captures — it produces logically sound data, but only within the boundaries of the TPTP system, and the definition of “interesting” theorems may skew the challenges. Moreover, benchmarks built from formal logic may not directly translate to how humans reason about mathematics in less rigid settings.
> 
> Even with those caveats, the implications are significant. The project not only provides a diagnostic tool to pinpoint where current AI systems fall short but also delivers a scalable pipeline for generating the kind of symbolic data that could help them improve. By openly releasing the code and data, the team invites the community to build on this work, potentially accelerating progress toward AI that can reason with rigor as well as fluency. More broadly, it nudges the field closer to uniting symbolic approaches with neural ones — a longstanding vision for achieving truly intelligent systems.

> What is Prophet Arena?
> 
> Prophet Arena is a new platform designed to test how well AI systems can forecast real-world events — not just recall facts. 
> 
> Rather than evaluating models using old, fixed datasets, Prophet Arena uses unfolding, real events and asks: *Can AI predict what happens next?*
> 
> What makes it special
> 
> The authors highlight several key features that distinguish Prophet Arena from existing benchmarks:
> 
> 1. Live Real-World Events
> 
>  It uses current, unresolved events (from prediction markets) so that models can’t “cheat” by having seen the answer already. 
> 
> 2. Probabilistic, Not Deterministic Predictions
> 
>  Instead of asking “which outcome is most likely,” AI models are asked to assign probabilities to all possible outcomes. This allows for measuring not just whether predictions are accurate, but how confident the AI was and how well calibrated its uncertainty is. 
> 
> 3. Human-AI Collaboration
> 
> Prophet Arena encourages interaction: humans can supply news or evidence, see how that affects the AI’s prediction, read its reasoning, and compare it with market data. 
> 
> 4. Transparent, Continuous Evaluation
> 
> There’s a leaderboard that tracks model performance over time across domains (politics, sports, science, entertainment, economics). Multiple metrics are used to get a holistic picture: how accurate, confident, and useful the predictions are. 
> 
> How it works
> 
> Here’s the basic pipeline:
> 
> * Information Gathering: AI models get access to up-to-date news, market signals, other relevant documents. 
> 
> * Forecast Submission: Given the same information, models generate a probability distribution over outcomes plus a rationale (why they assigned those probabilities). 
> 
> * Outcome Resolution & Scoring: When the event’s outcome becomes known, predictions are evaluated. Key metrics include the *Brier score* (how well probabilities match the outcome) and *Average Return* (how much money you’d make betting using the forecasted probabilities). 
> 
> What they’ve learned so far
> 
> From their early data:
> 
> * Better calibration (assigning probabilities that match real outcomes) generally correlates with better returns, but there are interesting exceptions. For example, sometimes models that take riskier or less conventional bets make more money even if they’re not “most accurate” overall. 
> 
> * AI models differ significantly in how they reason: some are more conservative, others aggressive. They also vary in how much they trust market prices vs. new information. 
> 
> * A model named `o3-mini` is performing well under the average return metric, leveraging opportunities where it believes it has an “edge” over market consensus. 
> 
>  Why this matters
> 
> Prophet Arena seeks to push forward AI’s ability to anticipate rather than just explain. Better forecasting is important for many real-world uses – financial markets, policy decisions, science, etc. The platform aims to provide a more realistic, dynamic, and actionable test of AI: dealing with uncertainty, reasoning with fresh information, and working in the real world.

> # ------------------------ Feature building ------------------------
> 
> def build_features(df: pd.DataFrame, tau: float) -> pd.DataFrame:
>  rows = []
>  for _, ev in df.iterrows():
>  b, e = be_from_question(ev["question"])
>  b, e, d, a = qa_tuple(b, e)
>  rows.append({
>  "id": ev["id"],
>  "question": ev["question"],
>  "p_market": float(ev["p_market"]),
>  "p_qa": qa_harmonic_prior(b, e, tau=tau),
>  "logical_ok": qa_valid(b, e, d, a),
>  "resolved": bool(ev["resolved"]),
>  "outcome": ev["outcome"],
>  "b": b, "e": e, "d": d, "a": a
>  })
>  return pd.DataFrame(rows)
> 
> # ------------------------ CV tuning & bootstrap -------------------
> 
> def cv_splits(n:int, k:int) -> List[np.ndarray]:
>  idx = np.arange(n)
>  if n < k:
>  # leave-one-out
>  return [np.array([i]) for i in range(n)]
>  rng = np.random.default_rng(123)
>  rng.shuffle(idx)
>  folds = np.array_split(idx, k)
>  return folds
> 
> def tuned_brier_resolved(res_df: pd.DataFrame, w: float, tau: float) -> float:
>  """Cross-validated Brier with L2 penalty on w."""
>  n = len(res_df)
>  if n == 0: return np.nan
>  folds = cv_splits(n, K_FOLDS if n >= MIN_RES_FOR_KFOLD else n)
>  bs_accum = []
>  for hold in folds:
>  train_mask = np.ones(n, dtype=bool)
>  train_mask[hold] = False
>  train = res_df.iloc[train_mask]
>  test = res_df.iloc[hold]
> 
>  feats_tr = build_features(train, tau=tau)
>  feats_te = build_features(test, tau=tau)
> 
>  z_m_te = logit(feats_te["p_market"].values)
>  z_q_te = logit(feats_te["p_qa"].values)
>  p_h_te = inv_logit(w*z_m_te + (1-w)*z_q_te)
> 
>  bs = brier_score(p_h_te, feats_te["outcome"].values)
>  bs_accum.append(bs)
> 
>  cv_bs = float(np.mean(bs_accum))
>  # L2 penalty (keeps w from slamming to boundary)
>  penalized = cv_bs + L2_LAMBDA * (w - L2_W_CENTER)**2
>  return penalized
> 
> def tune_w_tau(resolved_raw: pd.DataFrame) -> Dict[str, float]:
>  if len(resolved_raw) == 0:
>  return {"w": 0.7, "tau": 1.0, "brier": np.nan}
> 
>  best = {"w": None, "tau": None, "brier": np.inf}
>  for tau in TAU_GRID:
>  for w in W_GRID:
>  bs = tuned_brier_resolved(resolved_raw, w=w, tau=tau)
>  if not np.isnan(bs) and bs < best["brier"]:
>  best = {"w": float(w), "tau": float(tau), "brier": float(bs)}
>  return best
> 
> def bootstrap_ci_brier(p_pred: np.ndarray, y: np.ndarray, n_boot=BOOTSTRAP_N, seed=7):
>  rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
>  mask = ~np.isnan(y)
>  p = p_pred[mask]; o = y[mask]
>  if len(o) == 0:
>  return (np.nan, np.nan, np.nan)
>  n = len(o)
>  stats = []
>  for _ in range(n_boot):
>  idx = rng.integers(0, n, size=n)
>  stats.append(np.mean((p[idx]-o[idx])**2))
>  lo, hi = np.percentile(stats, [5,95])
>  return (float(np.mean(stats)), float(lo), float(hi))
>

>

> 
>  # Tune
>  best = tune_w_tau(resolved_raw)
>  w_opt, tau_opt = best["w"], best["tau"]
>  log(f"[tuning] w*∈[{W_MIN},{W_MAX}], τ∈[{TAU_GRID.min():.2g},{TAU_GRID.max():.2g}] -> "

>  log("[plot] saved qaforecast.png")
>  except Exception as e:
>  log(f"[warn] plotting failed: {e}")
> 
>  return {
>  "resolved_count": len(resolved_raw),
>  "unresolved_count": len(unresolved_raw),

>  return float(np.mean((preds - outcomes) ** 2))

> def qa_tuple(b: int, e: int):

>  a = b + 2*e
>  return b, e, d, a

> def qa_valid(b: int, e: int, d: int, a: int, tol=1e-12) -> bool:
>  return abs(a*a - (d*d + 2*d*e + e*e)) < tol
> 
> def qa_residue24(x: int) -> int:
>  return (x % 24) if math.gcd(x, 24) == 1 else (x % 8)
>

>  b24, e24 = qa_residue24(b), qa_residue24(e)
>  cos_phase = np.cos(np.pi * (b24 + e24) / 12.0)
>  z = cos_phase / max(tau, 1e-6)
>  p = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-z))
>  return float(0.5 + 0.9*(p - 0.5))

>  s = sum(ord(ch) for ch in text if ch.isalnum())

>  e = (len(unique) % 23) + 1

>  cursor = None

>  if not data:
>  break

>  "outcome": 1 if res=="YES" else 0,
>  "closeTime": int(close_time) if close_time is not None else None,

> # ====================== Tuning & backtesting =======================
> 
> def cv_splits(n:int, k:int) -> List[np.ndarray]:
>  idx = np.arange(n)
>  if n < k:
>  return [np.array([i]) for i in range(n)]
>  rng = np.random.default_rng(123); rng.shuffle(idx)
>  folds = np.array_split(idx, k)
>  return folds

> def tuned_brier_cv(df: pd.DataFrame, w: float, tau: float) -> float:
>  n = len(df)
>  if n == 0: return np.nan
>  k = K_FOLDS if n >= MIN_RES_FOR_KFOLD else n
>  folds = cv_splits(n, k)
>  bs_acc = []
>  for hold in folds:
>  train_mask = np.ones(n, dtype=bool); train_mask[hold] = False
>  tr = df.iloc[train_mask]; te = df.iloc[hold]
>  feats_te = build_features(te, tau=tau)
>  z = logit(feats_te["p_close"].values) * w + logit(feats_te["p_qa"].values) * (1-w)
>  p_h = inv_logit(z)
>  bs = brier_score(p_h, feats_te["outcome"].values)
>  bs_acc.append(bs)
>  cv_bs = float(np.mean(bs_acc))
>  return cv_bs + L2_LAMBDA * (w - L2_W_CENTER) ** 2

>  return {"w": 0.7, "tau": 1.0, "brier": np.nan}

>  for w in W_GRID:
>  bs = tuned_brier_cv(train_df, w=w, tau=tau)
>  if not np.isnan(bs) and bs < best["brier"]:
>  best = {"w": float(w), "tau": float(tau), "brier": float(bs)}
>  return best
>

>  bs_h_te = brier_score(p_h_te, feats_te["outcome"])

>  "w": w, "tau": tau,
>  "train": (feats_tr, p_h_tr, bs_m_tr, bs_h_tr),
>  "test": (feats_te, p_h_te, bs_m_te, bs_h_te)
>  }

>  "p_market": feats["p_close"].iloc[0],

>  "outcome": feats["outcome"].iloc[0],
>  "closeTime": feats["closeTime"].iloc[0]
>  })
