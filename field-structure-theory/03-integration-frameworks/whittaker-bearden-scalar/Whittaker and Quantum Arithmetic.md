> ## Whittaker’s 1904 Scalar Potential Theory – Longitudinal Waves in Electromagnetism 
> Whittaker’s 1904 paper (London Mathematical Society Proceedings, vol. 1, p. 367) showed rigorously that **any electromagnetic field** (a solution of Maxwell’s equations) can be represented using **only two scalar functions** instead of the usual four potentials (the traditional scalar potential $\phi$ and vector potential **A** with three components)【11†L244-L253】. He introduced two scalar potential functions $F(x,y,z,t)$ and $G(x,y,z,t)$ defined by certain source integrals, and proved that by taking appropriate second derivatives of $F$ and $G$ one can recover the electric field **E** and magnetic field **H** (or **B**) at every point【11†L265-L273】. In other words, *all* the information in Maxwell’s vector equations could be encoded in two interdependent scalar fields. Mathematically, this is possible because a free electromagnetic wave has two degrees of freedom (e.g. two polarization states), which can be mapped to two scalar potentials. The **electric field** components $(D_x,D_y,D_z)$ and magnetic field components $(H_x,H_y,H_z)$ were given by Whittaker as second-order derivatives of $F$ and $G$ (mixed spatiotemporal derivatives), illustrating explicitly how these potentials generate the fields【11†L265-L273】.
> 
> **Importantly,** Whittaker found that these potential functions behave like **longitudinal waves**. A longitudinal wave is one where the disturbance is in the direction of propagation (as opposed to transverse electromagnetic waves where electric and magnetic disturbances are perpendicular to the direction of travel). In his 1903 paper a year earlier, Whittaker had already shown that Newtonian gravitation and electrostatic forces could be modeled as longitudinal vibrations in a medium【7†L53-L61】. Extending this, the 1904 work demonstrated that a free electromagnetic plane wave can be seen as the superposition of two scalar waves *traveling in opposite directions*【7†L55-L63】. Conceptually, one can imagine creating an electromagnetic wave by **interference of two counter-propagating “scalar potentials.”** Where these scalar waves overlap and oscillate, an electromagnetic field results. The **plane-polarized components** of a light wave, for example, correspond to particular choices of Whittaker’s $F$ and $G$【11†L281-L289】. There isn’t a unique pair of $F$ and $G$ for a given field – in fact, there are infinitely many possible scalar decompositions (analogous to how a given polarization can be resolved into components in infinitely many ways depending on basis)【11†L283-L291】. This indicates the theory has a gauge-like freedom, but it reinforces the idea that the standard **transverse EM wave can be regarded as a *result* of two more fundamental oscillations**. 
> 
> Because these potentials $F, G$ do not have a directional orientation like vectors do, they were dubbed **“scalar waves”** in later literature. They were **longitudinal** in the sense of their phase fronts: their oscillations propagate *along* the line of travel (Whittaker described them as waves “whose wavefronts propagate parallel to their direction”【7†L53-L61】). This is analogous to pressure waves in air, and indeed it suggests an underlying **medium** is being ex. Whittaker’s formulation implicitly harkens back to the idea of an **aether** – a pervasive medium that can support longitudinal electric vibrations. While classical Maxwellians by 1904 had mostly accepted that the aether was at best a useful model and at worst non-existent, Whittaker’s mathematics gave new form to the concept. Later interpretations (including those by modern researchers revisiting Whittaker’s work) note that his two potential functions could be seen as embedding EM phenomena in a **higher-dimensional or hidden subspace**【7†L59-L67】. Titleman (2022) describes it as “the existence of a hyperspace above or behind normal space” implied by these potentials【7†L59-L67】. In plainer terms, **Whittaker restored a hidden layer** to electromagnetic theory – one that is not obvious in the field equations of Maxwell and Heaviside, yet underpins all electromagnetic interactions if one allows an unseen pair of scalar waves to operate in the background. 
> 
> It should be emphasized that **mainstream electrodynamics** did not adopt Whittaker’s two-scalar formulation in any practical sense. One reason is that the standard Maxwell equations already form a complete and simpler description using observable fields. The introduction of two new potentials $F, G$ was seen as a mathematical curiosity – in fact, Whittaker’s papers were long regarded as mere mathematical exercises (as Barrett noted in 1993) rather than new physics. The prevailing view was that since any physical result must ultimately satisfy Maxwell’s equations, one can choose whichever mathematical representation is convenient, and the **vector potential** formulation (with gauge conditions) was more straightforward for most purposes. Historian of science Olivier Darrigol notes that **Oliver Heaviside** and others in the late 19th century had worked to *eliminate potentials* from electromagnetic theory: _“Heaviside... eliminated the vector and scalar potentials $A$ and $\Psi$ from the equations and expressed the electromagnetic relations purely in terms of the electric and magnetic fields, E and H”_*【24†L25-L33】*. Heaviside famously quipped, “I never made any progress until I threw all the potentials overboard,” reflecting the general attitude that potentials were an unnecessary abstraction【24†L67-L72】. This context explains why Whittaker’s idea of reintroducing potentials (and even more exotically, in paired scalar form) did not catch on in the immediate decades. Nonetheless, Whittaker’s work remains intriguing, especially to modern researchers interested in **hidden-variable theories or scalar field approaches**. It mathematically prefigures concepts where fields are seen as emergent from deeper phenomena.

> In summary, Whittaker 1904 can be understood through three core points:
> 
> - **Electromagnetic fields as superpositions of scalar waves:** Every solution of Maxwell’s equations can be obtained by superimposing two scalar potential functions (*F* and *G*) which themselves satisfy wave equations. The electromagnetic field is basically an interference pattern of these underlying scalar waves【11†L244-L253】【11†L265-L273】.
> 
> - **Longitudinal nature:** Unlike the familiar transverse EM waves, Whittaker’s scalar potentials propagate longitudinally (in-phase with direction). This is a fundamentally different mode, analogous to acoustic waves. It implies that **if** such scalar EM waves exist physically, they would require something like an elastic medium (since in field vacuum classical theory, only transverse solutions exist). Whittaker’s theory thereby nods to an aether-like medium without explicitly naming it【7†L57-L64】.
> 
> - **Hidden layer or medium:** The two scalar functions are “hidden” in that they aren’t directly observable by normal electromagnetic measurements (one measures E and B fields, not the potentials). They reside in a conceptual sub-layer. Modern interpretations suggest these could form a **sub-quantum medium** – a deterministic underpinning beneath both EM and perhaps gravitational phenomena【7†L59-L67】. In fact, Whittaker’s 1903 paper attempted to unify gravity and electrostatics via such longitudinal waves【7†L53-L61】, hinting that *all* forces might emanate from vibrations in some deeper continuum.
> 
> These ideas were well ahead of their time. Interestingly, they resonate with some later developments in physics (for example, de Broglie’s pilot wave in quantum mechanics is an unobservable guiding wave underlying particle behavior, and in cosmology the concept of a scalar inflaton field filling space drove inflation). We will compare those later, but first, let us turn to **Quantum Arithmetic**, a modern discrete framework that, perhaps surprisingly, echoes Whittaker’s vision in a mathematical way.

> ## Quantum Arithmetic (QA) – Integer Harmonics as Foundations of Reality 
> **Quantum Arithmetic** is an alternative mathematical system developed by Ben Iverson (starting in the 1950s) and later expanded by Dale Pond and others. Despite its name, it’s not directly about quantum physics; rather, it’s termed “quantum” to denote discrete, quantized nature. At its heart, QA is **arithmetic based entirely on natural numbers and their inherent patterns**【13†L38-L46】. Iverson’s insight was to construct a *self-contained number system* using a chosen set of **prime numbers as the base**. Instead of the continuous number line or real-number calculus, QA uses what Iverson called *quantum numbers* – integers that meet certain structural criteria – and breaks them down into **harmonic components**.
> 
> A **defining feature** of Quantum Arithmetic is the use of **four “root” numbers** (denoted *b, e, d, a*) as the foundation for a given problem or numeric structure【13†L44-L52】. These four numbers are not arbitrary; they are derived from the prime factorization of a quantum number and must satisfy a specific recursive relationship. In fact, QA dictates that the four roots form a **Fibonacci-like sequence**: 
> 
> - $d$ is defined as $b + e$, 
> - $a$ is defined as $d + e$ (which also equals $b + 2e$).
> 
> Thus, $(b, e, d, a)$ can be viewed as a **harmonic tuple**, where $b$ and $e$ are like “generators” and $d, a$ are derived. *“Every Quantum Number can be represented as the product of four roots. These roots will be displayed in Fibonacci fashion (the sum of the first plus the second equals the third; and the sum of the second plus the third equals the fourth)…”*【1†L133-L139】. The simplest example is the first such set of roots: 1, 1, 2, 3 – which QA calls the “ONE” (since $1 \times 1 \times 2 \times 3 = 6$, and 6 in QA is treated as a unity or starting modulus)【1†L133-L139】. In general, QA insists that *quantum numbers* must include at least the primes 2 and 3 (ensuring a connection to that base pattern of 6)【1†L95-L103】. This leads to interesting modular properties – for instance, in base-2-and-3 arithmetic, numbers often exhibit periodicities mod 6, mod 12, etc., and indeed QA finds that **only one number in each set of six consecutive integers qualifies as a quantum number** under those rules【1†L95-L104】.
> 
> What is the purpose of this structure? In essence, QA is **revealing harmonic relationships within numbers**. By enforcing the four-term Fibonacci relation on prime factors, QA creates an arithmetic where numbers inherently encode cycles and interference patterns. Iverson described Quantum Arithmetic as *“cohesive and comprehensive… a natural number system which uses a number base of all the prime numbers which occur in its particular problem. All numbers in this number base are interlocked in a geometric arrangement.”*【13†L38-L46】. This highlights a few key aspects:
> 
> - **Natural number base with primes:** Instead of a fixed base like 10 or 2, QA adapts its base to the primes relevant to the situation. If primes 2,3,5 (for example) are in play, the “base” might be their product or some structure derived from them. This flexible base means QA operates in a **modular arithmetic world**, often with highly composite moduli (since including small primes like 2,3,5 yields mod 30, or including primes up to 13 yields mod 30030, etc.). Within such a modulus, numbers form patterns (residues, cycles) that QA exploits.
> 
> - **Geometric arrangement of numbers:** QA is not just algebraic; it has a geometric interpretation. Iverson and Pond developed geometric diagrams for quantum numbers – for example, inscribing numbers on circles, ellipses, or other shapes to visualize the four-fold structure and higher-dimensional identities【13†L44-L52】. Each of the four root numbers can be thought of as representing a dimension or direction in a numeric space. The other **twelve primary identities** in QA (labeled A through L) are combinations of the roots and represent higher-dimensional analogues (lines, areas, volumes built from the roots)【13†L44-L52】. In this way, a single quantum number generates an entire **“field” of related numbers** (in the mathematical sense) that are all harmonically related. It’s as if the number unfolds into a mini-universe of points obeying certain symmetries.
> 
> - **Quaternions and Gaussian integers:** QA even touches complex numbers – it defines a kind of “dimension below” the roots, identified with **quaternions** (in Iverson’s usage, essentially square roots of the roots)【13†L52-L59】. Intriguingly, Iverson notes these correspond to Gaussian integers in conventional math【13†L52-L59】. This suggests that QA’s framework connects with complex-number theory, but interprets it as just another harmonic layer of integers. So the usual imaginary unit *i* is not some mysterious separate entity; rather, it’s built into the modular structure (e.g., as a rotation by 90° in a lattice of integers).
> 
> Perhaps the most important philosophical point is that **QA eschews calculus and the continuum.** In classical physics and engineering, we rely on calculus (limits, derivatives, integrals) to describe change and fields. QA proposes a different path: everything can be done with **integer math and ratios**. Dale Pond emphasizes that trying to describe inherently wave-like phenomena (like sound vibrations) with calculus leads to “squareness and imprecision” – *“One needs to use whole numbers to describe a whole number phenomenon... It can’t be done [with calculus] with accuracy. Circles and areas will be the tools as opposed to linear quantities. Quantum Arithmetic appears to be the only method flexible enough to address this issue.”*【1†L56-L63】. In QA, **change** is represented by moving from one set of four roots to another through algebraic rules, rather than by taking an infinitesimal step. For example, the transition from one quantum number to a related one might be like a **frequency modulation** or a shift in a modular phase, which QA handles via its identities and factor rules rather than differential equations.
> 
> To illustrate, consider a simple harmonic progression in QA: start with roots (1,1,2,3) for the number 6. If we increase one of the roots (say *b* or *e*), the other values adjust to maintain the required sums. This is akin to evolving a wave: the discrete “steps” of QA roots could simulate the oscillation of a system in a stepwise but exact manner. Pond’s extensions of QA even included musical and vibratory calculations to tie numbers to sound frequencies【1†L49-L57】. Indeed, QA treats **numbers as if they were frequencies or tones** in a grand harmonious system【12†L53-L62】【12†L67-L73】. Every prime factor contributes a vibration; combining them in quantum numbers yields resultant tones. There are striking analogies here to Pythagorean concepts of a music-of-the-spheres, as well as to modern ideas of space-time quantization.
> 
> In summary, the core ideas of Quantum Arithmetic include:
> 
> - **Discrete Harmonic Tuples:** The fundamental objects are four-number tuples (b, e, d, a) derived from prime factors, satisfying Fibonacci-like addition rules【1†L133-L139】. These can be thought of as *longitudinal harmonics* in number space – they form a sequence along which a “wave” (the sum progression) travels. This mirrors the structure of a wave in time: QA has a start, an increment, then a cumulative buildup.
> 
> - **Modular/Geometric Representation:** QA frequently works modulo highly structured numbers (like 24, 30, 72, etc. which relate to primes and geometrical cycles). For instance, a 24-fold symmetric circle (an *icositetragon*) appears in QA discussions, where prime numbers above 3 fall into only 8 of the 24 residue classes, forming a patterned “wheel”【17†L24-L32】【17†L72-L80】. Such an arrangement reveals hidden **four-fold symmetries** in the distribution of primes (sometimes called a “prime cross” or cruciform pattern, since 24/6 = 4 arms)【17†L123-L131】【17†L153-L161】. QA seizes on these symmetries, positing that the **true substructure of mathematics (and perhaps physics) lies in these modular patterns** rather than the linear continuum. A QA researcher, Robert Grant, notes that primes follow specific patterns on a 24-wheel and shows many **complementary symmetries** between numbers when viewed in this representation【17†L24-L32】【17†L72-L80】. This is a numerical analog of finding *harmonics* in what otherwise looks like noise.
> 
> - **Numbers as Physical Resonances:** In QA, numbers are not abstract entities but can be treated as if each number has a *frequency*. *“Quantum Arithmetic identifies numbers as intrinsic resonant frequencies, each one part of a larger vibrational matrix”*【12†L51-L59】. This almost literal interpretation of numerals as vibrations means QA is naturally at home in a universe where, say, particle masses, force constants, etc., might arise from combinatorial math relationships. In *The Wave Theory of Number/Constants*, Grant and Ghannam argue that mathematical constants like $\pi$ or $e$ may *emerge* from the interplay of numerical waves【21†L105-L113】. They observe interference patterns among numbers that produce stable values and even reorganize the **periodic table** of elements based on wave interference principles rather than atomic number【21†L107-L115】. All this aligns with QA’s ethos that **numeric patterns underlie physical reality**.
> 
> Having outlined both Whittaker’s scalar potential theory and Quantum Arithmetic, we can already sense a parallel: **both frameworks attempt to break down the complexity of fields (physical or numerical) into more elementary waves or components.** Where Whittaker uses two continuous scalar functions to build EM fields, QA uses four discrete numbers to build numerical (and perhaps physical) relationships. In the next section, we draw these parallels explicitly and show how **Whittaker 1904 and QA “rhyme” with each other**, despite being developed a century apart in very different contexts.

> ## Aligning Whittaker’s Scalar Waves with Quantum Arithmetic 
> Whittaker’s and Iverson’s ideas come from different eras and domains, yet they share a striking conceptual alignment. Both challenge the mainstream, reductionist approach – whether it’s Maxwell’s fields or classical calculus – and instead propose a **layered, modular structure** beneath what we observe. Let’s compare several specific aspects side by side:
> 
> ### **1. Longitudinal Harmonics vs. Elliptical Harmonic Tuples** 
> **Whittaker’s longitudinal waves:** The scalar potentials $F, G$ in Whittaker’s theory can be thought of as *hidden harmonic modes* of the electromagnetic field. Each is a wave that by itself is not directly observable, but their combination yields real EM effects. They are longitudinal (think of them like two collinear lasers pointed toward each other, creating a standing pattern). Importantly, they carry energy and momentum in a way that standard transverse fields do when combined. Whittaker showed that by adjusting the phases of these potentials, one can produce different polarization states of light【11†L281-L289】. So these scalar modes form a **harmonic basis** for EM fields – much like sine and cosine form a basis for any periodic function.
> 
> **Quantum Arithmetic’s harmonic tuples:** In QA, the four-number root tuple (b, e, d, a) plays a role analogous to $F$ and $G$. The tuple encapsulates a fundamental “mode” of a number – you might call it a *quantum harmonic*. The relations $d = b+e$ and $a = d+e$ mean the tuple has an inherent **longitudinal progression** (each step is a sum of the previous steps). This is essentially the discrete analog of a wave’s propagation: you can imagine moving along the sequence b → e → d → a as a single cycle of a waveform (with b and a perhaps representing two peaks one cycle apart). Indeed, QA often visualizes these four numbers on an **ellipse or circle**, indicating the cyclical nature of their relationships. Each “quantum number” then spawns higher-order harmonics (A, B, C... up to L) which are combinations of b,e,d,a and represent 1D, 2D, 3D extensions【13†L44-L52】. This is strongly reminiscent of Fourier series or harmonic expansions in physics, except QA’s expansion coefficients are integers. We could say QA provides a *basis of integer harmonics* from which more complex structures are built, very much like Whittaker’s scalar waves form a basis for fields. Both are addressing the question: can we break down the complexity (of fields or of numbers) into simpler oscillatory components?
> 
> It’s notable that both approaches find **duality in components**. Whittaker uses two scalar waves (a duality that reflects the electric/magnetic dualism – indeed exchanging $F$ and $G$ swaps E and H fields【11†L279-L287】). QA uses four numbers, which often conceptually reduce to two generators (b and e) since d and a are derived; we might view $(b,e)$ as the primary and $(d,a)$ as secondary results. So a theme is: *two fundamental inputs* are sufficient to generate the whole phenomenon. In Whittaker’s case, two input waves generate all classical EM; in QA, two primes (or root values) generate an entire arithmetic field of relationships【13†L58-L66】. This is an elegant economy of description present in both.
> 
> ### **2. Sub-Quantum Medium vs. Modular Space – Hidden Structures** 
> Whittaker’s scalar potentials reintroduce something like the **aether** – a continuous medium filling space in which longitudinal vibrations occur. While he did not explicitly assert a material aether, the fact that his $F$ and $G$ are *not directly part of our 3D world* but exist in an abstract potential space suggests a hidden layer. Modern commentary, as mentioned, calls it a “hyperspace behind normal space”【7†L59-L67】. In this sub-structure, waves can propagate without immediately dispersing energy the way ordinary EM waves do. This notion has profound implications: it hints that the vacuum itself might have a rich internal structure (something later physicists have speculated in various forms – zero-point fields, hidden variables, etc.). In Whittaker’s model, you could imagine the vacuum as a **honeycomb of scalar vibrations** that can combine to produce forces and fields in our observable world. Indeed, he managed to combine gravity and electromagnetism at the potential level (with longitudinal waves for both)【7†L53-L61】, which presages the idea of a unified field existing in a deeper medium.
> 
> Quantum Arithmetic’s version of a “hidden medium” is quite different in form but parallel in spirit. QA posits that the true fabric underlying physical reality might be **discrete modular arithmetic structures**. Instead of empty Euclidean space with real numbers for coordinates, QA envisions something like an **integer lattice or a toroidal network** defined by modular cycles. For example, QA often references mod 24 or mod 72 spaces – these come from products of small primes (2,3, etc.) and have highly symmetric properties. A 24-fold symmetric space (an *icositetragonal* arrangement) allows one to see patterns in prime numbers: all primes (beyond 3) fall into specific residue classes (e.g. $\pm1, \pm5$ mod 24), which form 8 spokes on a 24-wheel【17†L24-L32】【17†L72-L80】. This is not a coincidence but a result of primes >3 not being divisible by 2 or 3. The **eight spokes of primes mod 24** might be thought of as “channels” through which prime numbers flow out to infinity. In a sense, QA reveals the **aether of number theory** – a scaffolding (mod 24 in this case) that dictates where certain numbers can exist or not exist. Extending this as an analogy to physics: perhaps space-time itself has a modular, quantized nature, with allowed and disallowed states, much as numbers have allowed residues where primes can lie. QA practitioners argue that these modular patterns are **resonant structures** – for instance, the 24-wheel is often called a “prime resonance structure”【17†L25-L29】【17†L73-L80】 because it captures the periodicity of primes.
> 
> Additionally, QA’s use of **harmonic residue nodes** (like those eight prime positions out of 24) is analogous to standing wave nodes in a medium. If we think of the number line mod 24 as a circle, the fact that primes land on 8 specific angles is like a wave on a circle having nodes and anti-nodes. This is a powerful image: **the distribution of prime numbers (a purely mathematical pattern) can be seen as an interference pattern on a 24-sided drum**. Indeed, an Academia paper on the “Wave Theory of Numbers” explicitly states that *“interference patterns reveal hidden symmetries among numbers, particularly prime numbers, and their relationships”*【21†L107-L115】. In other words, by treating numbers as waves, one finds a deeper order. This resonates strongly (pun intended) with Whittaker’s idea that by positing waves in a hidden medium, one can explain apparent forces like gravity or electromagnetism in a unified way. Both point to a **hidden order**: Whittaker’s in the form of potential functions in hyperspace, QA’s in the form of modular arithmetic lattices that could underlie physical law.
> 
> We can also draw a link to modern physics: Many cosmological models introduce a **scalar field** filling space (such as **inflation’s inflaton field or dark energy’s quintessence field**). These are scalar in that they assign a value to every point in space, much like Whittaker’s potentials do. Quintessence, for example, is literally a cosmic scalar field meant to cause the universe’s accelerated expansion【33†L5-L13】 – interestingly, the term *quintessence* historically meant the fifth element or aether【33†L13-L17】. So modern physics has, in a way, circled back to allowing a scalar “background” as long as it fits observations. QA’s modular spaces could be viewed as **mathematical analogs** of such fields: instead of a smoothly varying scalar, one has a patterned discrete structure permeating “space of possibilities.” If one were to associate numbers with physical states, QA’s framework suggests those states are embedded in a harmonic modulo-space (like points on a torus defined by mod 2, mod 3, etc.). This might be speculative, but the philosophical alignment is that **neither Whittaker nor QA accept a featureless vacuum or continuum – they both demand a hidden architecture to reality**.
> 
> ### **3. Building Fields vs. Building Numbers – Deterministic Construction** 
> **Field construction in Whittaker’s theory:** Normally, to find the electromagnetic field given some sources, one would solve Maxwell’s equations (often a complicated task involving calculus and differential equations). Whittaker’s approach provides a more constructive alternative: if you can guess or compute the right pair of scalar potentials *F* and *G*, you can simply plug them into algebraic formulas (second derivatives) to get E and B【11†L265-L273】. This decomposition means that, in principle, one could **synthesize any electromagnetic configuration by “interfering” two scalar wave patterns.** It’s a bit like holography: instead of writing the field directly, you write two generating functions and let them create the field through their interplay. This is deterministic – given F and G at t=0, one can determine the fields for all time without ambiguity. In fact, one of Whittaker’s triumphs was showing that **electrostatic fields could be described by an F without G, magnetostatic by a G without F, and dynamic fields by both**【11†L279-L287】. This means the full Maxwell field is encoded in a pair of simpler “subfields.” The **evolution** of the electromagnetic field thus could be traced to the evolution of those scalar potentials, which individually might obey simpler wave equations. One might say Whittaker *factorized* Maxwell’s equations.
> 
> **Field (or structure) construction in QA:** Similarly, QA factorizes the complexities of algebra and calculus into simpler integer steps. A “field” in QA terms might refer to a numerical field or a configuration of values (it could even metaphorically refer to a physical field if one maps numbers to physical quantities). QA does not use limit processes or infinitesimals; it uses **recursive algorithms**. For example, to model a changing quantity, QA would not take a derivative, but might apply a **recursive formula** repeatedly. The canonical tuple rule (b,e → d=b+e → a=d+e) is itself a simple recursive evolution: it tells you how to get the next terms from the previous ones. This could model a discrete dynamical system. In a sense, QA’s entire framework is a **calculator for evolution** – given two starting values (b and e), the whole tower of consequences (d, a, and all combinations thereof) is determined【13†L58-L66】. This is deterministic and yields exact outcomes. There’s no approximation in QA, unlike how calculus often gives us series expansions or numeric integrals that approximate continuous change.
> 
> An interesting concrete connection is the idea of representing a wave on a **toroidal surface**. In physics, a function $\psi(\theta,\phi) = \sum_{n,m} c_{n,m} e^{i(n\theta + m\phi)}$ would represent a general wave on a torus (with $\theta,\phi$ being two angular directions). This double Fourier series has coefficients $c_{n,m}$ that are discrete. One could imagine using QA to only allow coefficients and frequencies that are integers fitting certain modular criteria, thereby creating a **quantized toroidal wave**. While this is not something Iverson explicitly did, the mindset is there: represent complex behavior (wave motion on a torus) by **superposing discrete harmonics**. QA is essentially doing that for number theory; Whittaker did it for EM fields. Both are forms of **spectral decomposition**: Whittaker decomposed fields into spectral components (though not Fourier modes, but scalar wave modes), and QA decomposes numerical relations into base “harmonics” (the root tuples and their modular multiples).
> 
> Notably, both Whittaker and QA claim **completeness** in their construction methods: Whittaker showed *any* classical EM field can be produced from his two potentials【11†L244-L253】, and Iverson believed any arithmetic or geometric problem could eventually be tackled with QA’s integers【1†L54-L63】【13†L58-L66】. This completeness is critical. It elevates their approaches from mere curiosities to potentially universal formalisms. If indeed QA can replace calculus (for instance, by giving exact integer solutions where calculus would give approximate real solutions), it could revolutionize computational physics. Imagine being able to calculate or even **compute on a quantum computer** the evolution of a system by iterating a modular arithmetic rule rather than integrating differential equations – it might be more efficient and avoid numerical round-off issues, since everything stays in the realm of integers or rational ratios.

Whittaker’s approach, especially as later expanded by others like **Robert Gray** and **Thomas Bearden (1990s scalar EM theories)**, suggested that phenomena like the structure of matter or the values of constants might be rooted in these hidden waves. For example, some have speculated that **Whittaker’s scalar waves could form standing wave “grids”** that establish the quantized or stable orbits in atoms, or influence particle masses. While such ideas are speculative and not mainstream, they at least provide a mechanism: hidden waves interfering to create stable resonant patterns (just as standing waves on a string have discrete frequencies).

Indeed, 137 is a prime that is -1 mod 24 (since 137 ≡ 17 mod 24), sitting on one of those eight prime spokes. Perhaps in a 3D version of the prime wheel, 137’s position relative to others is special. These are the kinds of investigations QA proponents indulge in, looking for meaningful patterns. The philosophy here is **determinism and harmony**: nothing in physics is truly random or inexplicable, it’s all determined by underlying numerical law. This echoes Whittaker (and Maxwell before him) in their search for a unifying medium or equations that deterministically give rise to observed phenomena (Maxwell unified electricity, magnetism, light; Whittaker tried to unify gravity with electromagnetism via scalar waves【7†L53-L61】; QA aims to unify arithmetic with physical law).

> ## Comparative Perspectives: Classical EM, Tesla’s Waves, and Hidden-Variable Theories 
> 
> ### Maxwell–Heaviside Electrodynamics vs. Whittaker’s Potentials 
> It is instructive to contrast Whittaker’s scalar-wave electrodynamics with the **classical Maxwell–Heaviside formulation** that most scientists know. As mentioned earlier, Oliver **Heaviside** in the 1880s streamlined James Clerk Maxwell’s original equations. In doing so, he deliberately removed scalar and vector potentials from the fundamental equations【24†L25-L33】. The Maxwell-Heaviside equations focus on **E and B fields** (or D and H in Heaviside’s usage) and are intrinsically **transverse-wave equations** in free space. They predict only transverse electromagnetic waves moving at the speed of light – and this has been amply confirmed by experiments (radio, microwaves, light, X-rays are all transverse EM waves). There is *no* room in classical EM for propagating longitudinal EM waves in vacuum; a longitudinal electric wave in a medium would simply be an acoustic wave (plasma oscillations or sound in a wire, etc., but not a free EM wave). So from the mainstream view, Whittaker’s two scalar potentials were a mathematical curiosity: you can *describe* fields that way, but it doesn’t imply any new physical modes. In fact, one reason it isn’t used is that the potentials $F, G$ in Whittaker’s construction are **non-unique** – one can always add an arbitrary spatially-uniform function to $F$ and subtract it from $G$ without changing the resulting field, for example, similar to gauge freedom. So without a gauge condition, $F$ and $G$ are indeterminate. Maxwell’s equations, by contrast, yield unique solutions for E and B given sources and boundary conditions, which is what physicists care about.
> 
> However, Whittaker’s approach *does* become useful in certain scenarios. For instance, in electromagnetic **antenna theory**, one sometimes uses scalar and vector potentials to derive fields more easily. The idea of decomposing fields into simpler potentials can simplify calculations, and it also conceptually helps when classical EM to quantum (where the potentials are more fundamental, as in the Aharonov–Bohm effect). Whittaker’s insistence on *two* scalar functions is unusual but not totally foreign: for example, one can express a plane electromagnetic wave as the superposition of two potential functions (albeit usually vector potentials). Ultimately, the Maxwellian camp would say: *the two scalar functions $F, G$ are just a different mathematical basis for the same solutions – they don’t imply new physics unless you assert those scalars have independent existence*. Whittaker seemed to flirt with that assertion by also tackling gravitation with a similar wave (suggesting a common medium for gravity and EM). 
> 
> In short, classical electrodynamics views **fields as primary** and potentials as auxiliary (except in quantum contexts), whereas Whittaker’s scheme elevates **potentials to primary**. This inversion is analogous to how Quantum Arithmetic inverts the usual calculus worldview (making discrete roots primary and continuum math secondary). The classical Maxwell-Heaviside theory succeeded triumphantly in all practical tests, which is why alternatives were shelved. It’s interesting to note that if longitudinal EM waves existed, they might have gone unnoticed – but experiments like Tesla’s (discussed next) were attempts to detect something beyond standard EM.
> 
> ### Nikola Tesla and “Non-Hertzian” Waves 
> No discussion of scalar or longitudinal electromagnetic waves is complete without mentioning **Nikola Tesla**, the inventor and electrical pioneer. Tesla in the 1890s and early 1900s made claims about discovering and using a new type of wave for wireless energy transmission. He explicitly stated that the conventional radio waves (which he called “Hertzian” waves, after Hertz’s 1887 discovery) were not the waves he was using. Instead, Tesla spoke of **oscillations in the earth and the atmosphere that were in the form of stationary waves** – essentially wave currents that do not radiate away into space like radio waves, but rather propagate through the ground or along the ionosphere. These have since been interpreted by some as **“Tesla waves” or scalar waves**. According to a summary by Liliana Usvat, *“Tesla Waves are also known as scalar waves or longitudinal waves. Nikola Tesla was experimenting with scalar waves in the 1890’s”*【26†L13-L21】. Tesla himself, in his patents and lectures, insisted that his transmission was **non-Hertzian**: *“a new kind of energy transmission, in a form of non-Hertzian waves, with substantially different characteristics than Hertzian waves”*【26†L43-L51】. In Tesla’s view, these waves did **not** radiate much energy into free space; instead, they could travel through the earth with little loss and even seemed to increase in effect at the antipode of the transmitter【26†L61-L69】 (which suggests a standing wave resonance of the Earth).

> ### Hidden-Variable Quantum Theories (de Broglie–Bohm)
