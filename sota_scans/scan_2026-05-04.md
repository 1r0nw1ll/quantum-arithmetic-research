### #1 — Drift-Free Conservative Dynamics from Quantized Interaction Rules
**Rating**: HIGH  
**Source**: arXiv:2604.26383 (April 29, 2026)  
**URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26383  
**Authors**: Park Junhu, Yongsoo Ha, Myungjoo Kang

**Summary**: Introduces an operator-level formulation of conservative dynamics as an exact discrete interaction rule on a quantized integer state space. The core construct is an antisymmetric integer-transfer operator: conservation is enforced exactly at the arithmetic level, eliminating round-off drift entirely. Shock structure selection (entropy condition) is achieved via monotone order-preserving transfer — no flux reconstruction or Riemann solver needed. Numerical experiments show high-frequency transport accurate near the Nyquist limit and sharply localized Burgers shocks. Extension to multidimensional systems and systems of conservation laws via oriented vector-valued integer transfers. Companion to arXiv:2604.06947.

**QA Mapping Potential**: This is the strongest new find. QA's state evolution (b,e) → ((b+e-1)%m+1, e) is precisely an integer-transfer operator: the new b' = (b+e-1)%m+1 is an exact modular integer computation, and QA's A1 (no zero), S2 (no float state), and T1 (path time = integer k) axioms collectively define the same class of "quantized state space with exact integer dynamics" the paper formalizes. The paper's "antisymmetric transfer" (conservation by antisymmetry) maps to QA's orbit reversibility (the T-operator is invertible over Z/mZ). The "continuum emerges via reconstruction" framing is precisely Theorem NT + T2: QA's observer layer is the reconstruction operator; the discrete layer is the quantized state. This paper provides a PDE-side proof-of-concept that the QA architecture is natural and necessary for exact conservation.

**Cert scaffolding candidate**: `qa_quantized_integer_dynamics_cert_v1` — formalize QA's step operator as an antisymmetric integer-transfer operator in the sense of arXiv:2604.06947 + arXiv:2604.26383. Key claim: QA's Cosmos orbit is a closed attractor of a monotone integer-transfer system; orbit membership is an integer-level invariant.

**Source**: arXiv:2604.06947 (April 8, 2026)  
**URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06947

### #3 — qFHRR: Rethinking Fourier Holographic Reduced Representations through Quantized Phase and Integer Arithmetic
**Rating**: HIGH  
**Source**: arXiv:2604.25939 (April 16, 2026)  
**URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25939  
**Authors**: Shay Snyder, Hamed Poursiami, Maryam Parsa (George Mason University)

**Summary**: Replaces the 64-bit complex floating-point vectors of FHRR (Fourier Holographic Reduced Representations — a vector-symbolic architecture for structured information) with discrete phase indices and integer-only modular arithmetic. Binding, unbinding, similarity, and bundling all execute via modular addition and lookup tables. Reduces from 64 bits/dimension to 3–4 bits while maintaining high fidelity. Achieves an algebraically exact group structure: the discrete phase indices form Z/N for various N, and binding is exactly modular addition.

**QA Mapping Potential**: Strong and specific. QA's state (b,e) with derived coords (d,a) is a 4-vector over Z/mZ where binding (derivation) is modular addition: d = b+e mod m, a = b+2e mod m. The "exact group structure" (Z/N, modular addition = binding) formally characterizes QA's derivation system. QA can be cast as a 4-dimensional modular VSA where the two "free" dimensions are b,e and the two "bound" dimensions d,a are derived. This gives QA a clean theoretical grounding in the VSA/HDC literature.

**Cert scaffolding candidate**: `qa_modular_vsa_cert_v1` — formalize QA's 4-tuple (b,e,d,a) as a modular composite representation over Z/24Z^4, prove that QA's derivation rules are VSA binding operations, and show that orbit membership is a VSA similarity predicate.

### #7 — Fibonacci Numbers and the Probability of Polygon Formation
**Rating**: LOW-MEDIUM  
**Source**: arXiv:2604.27573 (April 30, 2026)  
**URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27573  
**Authors**: Mark Brennan, Noah Callow, Tian Cao Lin

**Summary**: For n random sticks, the probability that no p+1 sticks form a (p+1)-polygon equals the product of reciprocals of a series of p-step Fibonacci number terms.
