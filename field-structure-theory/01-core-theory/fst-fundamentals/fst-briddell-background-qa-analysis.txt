Background and Development
    • Don Briddell began his structural studies and research in 1965 as an undergraduate at Pratt Institute in New York City[4].
    • He met with Buckminster Fuller in 1966, the same year he graduated with a degree in industrial design[4].
    • Briddell continued to work independently on Field Structure Theory and made his work public in 2004 with a presentation at a meeting of the Synergetics Collaborative[4].
Key Concepts
    1. Field-Centric Approach: FST posits that the field is responsible for form, not the other way around. This contrasts with traditional physics approaches that study objects to understand fields[4].
    2. Loops of Action: FST proposes that fields are composed of interacting loops of action that produce form in three dimensions[4].
    3. No Intersections: In FST, loops or lines interact but do not intersect. Briddell argues that everything in the natural universe is interacting but not intersecting[4].
    4. Tometry: Briddell developed a new geometry called Tometry, which is based on natural structure and produced the first true three-dimensional knot, called a Fieldstructure (FS)[6].
Applications
    1. Particle Physics: Briddell has applied FST to explain the particle hierarchy in physics. His book "The Particle Hierarchy Paradigm: According to Field Structure Theory" explores this application[1][8].
    2. Energy Generation: Briddell is developing field energy electron generation and storage devices based on FST[2].
Institutional Work
    • In 2020, Briddell founded the Field Structure Institute to promote research into field structures and provide a platform for those interested in "Structural Physics"[2].
    • He also founded FieldStructure.org in 2004 to engage in research, teaching, and applications of Field Structure Theory[2].
Relation to Other Theories
    • FST is presented as an alternative to some aspects of String Theory, particularly in its approach to closed loops rather than open strings[4].
    • Briddell's work appears to be influenced by Buckminster Fuller's ideas, as he considers himself a student of Fuller[2].
While Field Structure Theory represents an innovative approach to understanding fundamental physics and structure, it's important to note that it is not yet widely accepted in mainstream physics and requires further peer review and validation within the scientific community.
