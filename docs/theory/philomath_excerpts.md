## Part I — Math & Numbers

### #digital-root-definition (PDF p.16)

> "The digital root, also known as modular math, is one important branch of number theory... In simple words, what the digital root operation does is to reduce a number down to a single digit. This is done by continuously summing the number's individual digits."

### #base10-is-base9 (PDF p.16)

> "In this book, however, we concentrate mainly on base-10 (which is actually base-9, as explained below.) ... The possible outcome of this operation can be any number from 1 to 9 only (this is why we suggested it is more of a base-9 rather than base-10)."

### #nine-is-zero-of-dspace (PDF p.19)

> "Even though number 9 is the last and largest number of the D-space, it behaves in this finite space as number 0 does in the regular infinite numerical space. For example, adding 9 to any number will not change its digital root... This property of number 9, being the largest and smallest at the same time, is unique to the D-space."

### #septenary-cycle (PDF p.19)

> "Dividing by 7 is a special case, where the result is always a repetitive sequence of the numbers: [1, 4, 2, 8, 5, 7]. These six digits [1, 4, 2, 8, 5, 7] are called the Septenary Numbers, and we will be encountering them a lot throughout the book."

### #72-year-precession (PDF p.19-20) — [KNOWN TYPO]

> "Every 72 years (D = 9), the zodiacs move one degree from their previous year's position. For a full cycle, it takes around 25920 years (360×72), and D(28920) = 9."

**Typo note:** the book prints `D(28920) = 9` at the end. The correct value is `D(25920) = 9` (25920 was the intended referent, matching `360×72=25920`); `D(28920)` in fact equals 3. The SourceClaim preserves the verbatim text; a separate observation node records the correction.

### #prime-form-6k-pm-1 (PDF p.29)

> "Mathematically, prime numbers always come in the form of [6k±1], where k is any integer from 1 to infinity... From this formula, it appears that number 6 is a pivot or a center number around which prime numbers emerge. This specific role of number 6 is so fundamental that it will be at the core of some of this book's most important theories."

### #prime-square-mod-24 (PDF p.29)

> "One such interesting property is that for any prime number p, its square is always a multiple of 24, plus 1 (p^2 = 24f + 1)... If we combine [the coefficient] into a single factor f, we retrieve the desired equation: p^2 = 24f + 1."

### #prime-digital-root-constraint (PDF p.29-30)

> "In the D-space, with the exception of number 3, the digital root of any known prime number is anything but 3, 6, or 9... no prime number can have a digital root of 3, 6, or 9."

### #vedic-square-mirror (PDF p.31)

> "Shapes that correspond to numbers adding up to 9, as [2, 7], [1, 8], etc., are mirror images of each other. Tracing number 9 generates a square, which is its own mirror image."
