Subject: Arithmetic structure invisible to geometric metrics — connection to your NAACL prompting geometry work

Hi Artem,

I came across your NAACL 2025 paper with SueYeon Chung on the
geometry of prompting — the result that different prompting strategies
induce distinct representational geometries in LLMs is striking. I
wanted to flag a finding from a different direction that raises a
question I think your toolkit is uniquely positioned to investigate.

I work on Quantum Arithmetic (QA), a modular arithmetic framework.
The setup is simple: integer pairs (b, e) mod m generate 4-tuples
(b, e, d, a) where d = b+e, a = b+2e. For m = 9, the 81-element
state space self-classifies into three orbit types under iteration:
72 elements in a 24-cycle ("Cosmos"), 8 in an 8-cycle ("Satellite"),
and 1 fixed point ("Singularity").

Here is what's interesting about the representational geometry of
these tuples:

Standard metrics see NOTHING. The 4D tuple space has:
  - Participation ratio = 4.00/4.00 (perfectly isotropic)
  - SVD spectrum = [0.250, 0.250, 0.250, 0.250] (perfectly flat)
  - Cosine gap between orbit classes = -0.034 (NEGATIVE — between-
    class pairs are MORE similar than within-class)
  - Fisher ratio = 1.36 (barely above chance)

Every standard representational geometry tool reports "no structure"
or "isotropic."

But an arithmetic decoder sees PERFECT structure. The 3-adic
valuation of the quadratic norm f(b,e) = b^2+be-e^2 classifies
orbits with zero error:
  - v_3(f) = 0 → Cosmos (72/72 correct)
  - v_3(f) = 2 → Satellite (8/8 correct)
  - v_3(f) = 4 → Singularity (1/1 correct)

When we tested "arithmetic attention" (selecting neighbors by v_3
proximity) vs cosine attention (selecting by angle): v_3 attention
retrieves same-orbit neighbors 100% of the time. Cosine attention
retrieves 64% — worse than the 89% random baseline. Cosine
anti-selects.

The algebraic reason: f(b,e) is the norm in the golden ratio integer
ring Z[phi], and the orbit structure of Z[phi]/9Z[phi] is determined
by 3-adic ideal factorization. The classification is number-theoretic,
not geometric — no embedding metric can recover it because the
structure lives in divisibility, not direction.

This raises a specific question for neural representations: are
there cases where activations appear isotropic under PCA/cosine/
clustering, but reveal structure under arithmetic operations
(modular residues, p-adic valuations, divisibility patterns)? If
standard tools are systematically blind to arithmetic encoding,
that's a gap in the field's analysis toolkit.

I don't know the answer, but it seems like the kind of question
your statistical physics framework could test — you already analyze
how representational geometry depends on the method used to probe it.
